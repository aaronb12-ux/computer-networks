## The Network Edge

### Access Networks and Physical Media

The network that connects the end systems: the host, device into the larger global internet.

**3 Types of Access Networks:**
- Residential access networks
- Institutional access networks (school, company)
- Mobile access networks (WiFi, 4G/5G)

**What to look for:**
- Transmission rate (bits per second) of access network?
- Shared or dedicated access among users?

### Access Networks: Cable-Based Access

A physical cable connects multiple homes to a single cable headend.

Signals are sent in different frequencies.

**Frequency Division Multiplexing (FDM):** different channels transmitted in different frequency bands. We tune into the frequency that we want.

Cable access networks are typically **asymmetric**, meaning they are designed to transmit data faster in the downstream direction towards the home rather than upstream from the home.

**HFC: Hybrid Fiber Coax**
- Up to 40 Mbps - 1.2 Gbps downstream transmission rate
- 30-100 Mbps upstream transmission rate
- Network of cable, fiber attaches homes to ISP router
- Homes **share** access network to cable headend

### Access Networks: Digital Subscriber Line (DSL)

- Use existing telephone line to central office DSLAM
- Data over DSL phone line goes to internet
- Voice over DSL phone line goes to telephone network

Are also **asymmetric:**
- 24-52 Mbps **dedicated** downstream transmission rate
- 3.5-16 Mbps **dedicated** upstream transmission rate
- These rates depend on the distance between the central office and your home

### Wireless Access Networks

Shared wireless access network connects end system to router via base station aka 'access point'.

**Two classes of wireless networks:**
- **Local wireless networks (WiFi):** typically 10-100 Mbps, range: tens of meters
- **Wide-area cellular access networks (3G/4G/5G):** provided by mobile network operator, range: tens of kilometers

### Host: Sends Packets of Data

**Host sending function:**
1. Takes application message
2. Takes data it wants to send and breaks it into smaller chunks of data known as **packets** of length L bits
3. There's also a packet header, and a protocol will determine what information is added into this header
4. The host then transmits this L-bit packet into the access network at transmission rate R measured in bits/second
   - R is best thought of as a link transmission rate

**Packet transmission delay** = time needed to transmit L-bit packet into link = **L (bits) / R (bits/sec)**
